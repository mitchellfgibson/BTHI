<p>These are not aspirations. They are the structural choices we've made about how this platform operates, and why. Each one costs something — either in revenue, in deal flow, or in operational complexity. We're making them anyway, because the alternative is becoming something we wouldn't invest in ourselves.</p>

<hr class="rule">

<ol class="principles-list">

  <li class="principle-item">
    <div class="principle-number">01</div>
    <div class="principle-body">
      <h3>We co-invest on every deal, on the same terms.</h3>
      <p>The platform's balance sheet goes into every deal we list, alongside the community and the lead investor. Not a nominal check. Not a carried-interest-only arrangement. Actual capital at risk on the same terms as every other investor in the round.</p>
      <p>If we don't believe in a deal enough to put our own money in it, we don't list it. This is the OurCrowd model applied to non-accredited co-investment. It is the single most important structural commitment we make, because it changes what it means to evaluate a deal. A platform that earns listing fees has an incentive to list. A platform that co-invests has an incentive to be right.</p>
      <p>The practical consequence: we will list fewer deals than our competitors. Fewer deals means less fee revenue in the short term. We think this is the right trade.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="principle-item">
    <div class="principle-number">02</div>
    <div class="principle-body">
      <h3>Every fee is published, on every deal, at the deal level.</h3>
      <p>We charge two things: a flat 2% from the issuer (paid by the company, not the investor) and a 10% carry to the lead investor on member gains, payable only if the investment is positive. That's the full fee structure. It is published on every deal page, in plain language, before you commit a dollar.</p>
      <p>No tiered marketing packages. No paid promotion. No "featured deal" slots that issuers can buy. No hidden spreads on the investment price. No subscription fees that create a two-tier member experience.</p>
      <p>We have studied the Linqto reseller model, where investors pay an undisclosed markup above fair market value. We have studied platforms where companies can pay to be promoted higher in the deal feed. We are not doing either of those things. If a company asks us for a paid promotion slot, we will decline and note the request in the deal file.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="principle-item">
    <div class="principle-number">03</div>
    <div class="principle-body">
      <h3>Members get the same terms as the lead investor.</h3>
      <p>In standard Reg CF practice, retail investors get a stripped-down version of the deal. No pro-rata rights on the next round. No information rights. No governance on major events. The lead investor and institutional co-investors negotiate those rights in side letters that retail investors never see.</p>
      <p>We do not do this. Members investing on this platform receive: pro-rata rights on the next priced round, quarterly investor updates, voting rights on major events (sale, dissolution, significant asset transfer), and full information rights equivalent to the lead's. The legal architecture required to deliver these rights to non-accredited investors in a Reg CF structure is more complex than the standard approach. We have done the work to make it functional.</p>
      <p>When we say "same terms," we mean it literally. If the lead receives a right that members don't, we don't list the deal.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="principle-item">
    <div class="principle-number">04</div>
    <div class="principle-body">
      <h3>Diligence is real, published, and disputable.</h3>
      <p>Every deal that appears on this platform has a diligence scorecard from the lead investor, with specific rationale for each criterion. The scorecard is published alongside the deal, not summarized or abstracted. Members can read the exact reasoning the lead investor used and disagree with it in the community Q&amp;A thread.</p>
      <p>We do not moderate critical commentary out of deal threads. If a member finds a problem with the lead's valuation comp set, that comment stays visible. If the lead's response is inadequate, that's visible too. Member-led skepticism is the system working, not a bug in the community.</p>
      <p>We will, over time, publish our own diligence track record — how our assessments at the time of investment compared to outcomes. We will do this including the deals that fail, not just the ones that succeed.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="principle-item">
    <div class="principle-number">05</div>
    <div class="principle-body">
      <h3>Bad deals don't get listed, regardless of the fee.</h3>
      <p>The first time we list a deal because the company paid us more, or because we needed to hit a quarterly deal volume target, is the day we become Wefunder. We won't.</p>
      <p>This principle requires that we be willing to say no to deals that would generate meaningful fee revenue. It requires that the person making the listing decision is not compensated on deal volume. It requires that we publish our rejection rate, so members can assess whether our selectivity is real. We intend to do all three of these things.</p>
      <p>What "passing our diligence" means in practice: the lead investor has skin in the game (real capital at risk, not just a nominal check), the terms are equivalent across all investor classes, and the risk disclosures on the deal page accurately represent the risks as we understand them — including risks the company would prefer not to highlight.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="principle-item">
    <div class="principle-number">06</div>
    <div class="principle-body">
      <h3>The community is the product. Not the deal flow.</h3>
      <p>Most retail investment platforms treat their members as a distribution channel — a pool of capital that issuers pay to access. The member is the product being sold to the issuer. The issuer is the customer.</p>
      <p>We have inverted this. Our members are the customer. Issuers are the supplier. The community's analytical capacity — the ability to identify problems in a deal scorecard, to surface public information about a lead investor's track record, to ask the question that nobody on the cap table has asked — is the thing that makes deals on this platform worth investing in.</p>
      <p>The practical consequence of this commitment: we will never limit critical commentary to protect a deal. We will never add a paywall that restricts the research desk to premium members. We will never structure our deal feed to prioritize issuers who pay more. The community's trust is the only asset that matters.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

</ol>

<hr class="rule">

<div style="margin: 2.5rem 0 1rem;">
  <span class="kicker">Commitments</span>
  <h2>What we won't do</h2>
  <p>Five things we commit to never doing. These are published here so that if we ever do them, members can hold us to this page.</p>
</div>

<ul class="wont-list">
  <li class="wont-item">
    <div class="wont-item-text">
      <strong>Paid promotion or featured deal placement.</strong>
      <span>Companies cannot pay to appear higher in the deal feed, be featured in member emails, or be marked as "editor's pick." Deal placement is determined by open date only.</span>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li class="wont-item">
    <div class="wont-item-text">
      <strong>Accredited-only tiers with better deal access.</strong>
      <span>Every member sees every deal. We will not create a premium membership tier where accredited investors get earlier access, better terms, or exclusive deal flow. That's called a platform with two classes of investors.</span>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li class="wont-item">
    <div class="wont-item-text">
      <strong>Carry on deals where we don't co-invest.</strong>
      <span>We only earn carry on deals where we've put platform capital at risk. We will never structure an arrangement where we earn carry without co-investing. That's what alignment means.</span>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li class="wont-item">
    <div class="wont-item-text">
      <strong>Secondary market spreads or liquidity theater.</strong>
      <span>We will not build a secondary market that implies liquidity we can't deliver. If we eventually facilitate secondary trading, we will publish bid-ask spreads and volume data in real time, and we will not earn a spread on trades.</span>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li class="wont-item">
    <div class="wont-item-text">
      <strong>Investor-count marketing without outcome disclosure.</strong>
      <span>We will not say "join 50,000 investors" without also publishing what those investors' returns have been. Member count without return data is marketing. We are not a marketing operation.</span>
    </div>
  </li>
</ul>

<div class="closing-block">
  <p>These principles were written before the platform has a single live deal, a single paying member, or a single dollar of revenue. That's deliberate. It's easier to commit to structural choices when they haven't cost you anything yet. The test of these principles is whether they hold when they do cost something — when a high-fee deal wants a listing and the answer is no, when a member's critical comment might discourage an investment and we publish it anyway, when the deal volume target creates pressure to list something we shouldn't.</p>
  <p>If you see us violating any of these commitments, we want to know. The Q&amp;A threads on every deal page are unmoderated. So is our inbox.</p>
</div>
