<div class="deal-section">
  <span class="deal-section-label">The thesis</span>
  <h2>Why I backed this</h2>
  <p>Common Ground isn't the best coffee business I've seen. It's the most honest one. I ran Equator Coffees from 12 employees to 7 locations and a PE exit, and I'm the first to tell you what went wrong there: our worker equity program was aspirational rather than structural. The employees got a party and some bonuses at exit. Common Ground's model gives the first five employees 15% of the equity at founding — not as a retention mechanism, but as a structural commitment that the people running the stores share in what they build.</p>
  <p>The $12M cap is the number I'll get questions about, and I want to answer them before they're asked. At Equator's exit in 2021, the company had $4.2M in revenue across 7 locations and was valued at roughly $4M by the acquirer. Common Ground has $2.8M in revenue across 6 locations. On pure multiple math, $12M looks expensive. My thesis is that the premium is justified by two things: Common Ground's direct-trade supplier relationships produce gross margins 8–10 points above Equator's at a comparable stage, and the worker equity structure has driven 14% annual staff turnover against a specialty coffee industry average above 70%. Turnover is the unit economics killer in this business. Common Ground has largely solved it.</p>
  <p>My investment in one sentence: if the worker equity model drives the unit economics it appears to drive, $12M is reasonable; if it doesn't, this is overpriced and I'm wrong about the thesis.</p>
  <div class="thesis-sig">— Marcus Webb, Former CEO, Equator Coffees &nbsp;·&nbsp; Investing $150,000 on these terms</div>
</div>

<div class="deal-section">
  <span class="deal-section-label">Why this deal exists here</span>
  <h2>Why Common Ground is raising on Unaccredited</h2>
  <p>Common Ground's customer base is its investor thesis. The company operates six locations across three mid-size Pacific Northwest cities. Their regulars — the people who pay $5.50 for a direct-trade latte three times a week — are exactly the community stakeholders the founders want as shareholders. These are not accredited investors. They're people who show up at 7am and know the names of the staff because the same people are there every morning. The company chose Reg CF because that's the only legal mechanism to sell them equity.</p>
  <p>We listed this deal because: (1) Marcus Webb is investing $150,000 of his own capital at the same terms as every community investor — not a nominal check, not from a fund; (2) the direct-trade supplier agreements and the worker equity structure are documented and verifiable in the Form C; and (3) the company passed our diligence process, which means the lead investor is on record with a rationale that members can interrogate. We are investing $25,000 of platform capital alongside the community. If Common Ground fails, we lose too.</p>

  <h2>What can go wrong</h2>
  <p style="font-size:0.88rem; opacity:0.75; max-width:60ch;">These are not boilerplate. They are the specific risks we think are most likely to matter for this investment.</p>
</div>

<div class="risk-section">
  <ol class="risk-list">
    <li class="risk-item">You can lose all of your money. Common Ground is a multi-location food and beverage business in a category with a high base-rate failure. The most likely outcome for any single business of this size is not a profitable exit — it is a slow revenue decline, a forced sale below the amount raised, or a wind-down. Treat that as the base case.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">This investment is illiquid. There is no secondary market for Reg CF equity. You cannot sell your position until a company acquisition, IPO, or dissolution — none of which you control, none of which are guaranteed, and all of which are at minimum 5–7 years away.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">The unit economics thesis may not survive scale. The claim that worker equity reduces turnover, and that turnover reduction drives a lasting margin advantage, is supported by the data at 6 locations. At 12 or 20 locations, operational complexity increases, the founders become less visible, and structural advantages that hold at small scale often erode. This is the central investment risk.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">Consumer discretionary exposure. A specialty coffee chain charging premium prices is a discretionary expense. Economic contraction, sustained inflation in coffee commodity prices, or a sustained shift in consumer behavior could materially reduce same-store sales. There is no recurring revenue, no contract, and no switching cost that protects the business in a downturn.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">Direct-trade supplier concentration. A meaningful portion of the margin advantage depends on five direct-trade supplier relationships. Climate disruption, geopolitical instability in producing regions, or the loss of a key farm relationship could move COGS materially and compress the margins that justify the valuation.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">The lead investor is not your fiduciary. Marcus Webb's interest in Common Ground's success is aligned with yours in a general sense, but his exit preferences, portfolio construction, and carry incentive may differ from yours in specific decisions. The 10% carry is the only formal alignment mechanism, and it applies only to gains.</li>
  </ol>
