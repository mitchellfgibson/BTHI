<div class="deal-section">
  <span class="deal-section-label">The thesis</span>
  <h2>Why I backed this</h2>
  <p>I spent 22 years at Frontier Communications, the last five as VP of Operations for the Mountain West region. I know exactly what rural broadband looks like from the inside of a large incumbent carrier: it looks like a series of decisions to under-invest in areas where the economics are marginal and the regulatory pressure is low. The carriers are not evil. They are rational. The ARPU on a 40-household rural route doesn't justify the maintenance costs on copper-pair infrastructure that should have been replaced fifteen years ago. So it doesn't get replaced.</p>
  <p>LastMile's thesis is that fixed-wireless technology has finally reached the point where the economics work for a well-run regional operator that a large incumbent cannot match on cost structure. Their network deployment costs are approximately 60% below what Frontier would spend for comparable coverage, because they're not maintaining legacy infrastructure and they're not carrying a corporate cost structure designed for a $5B company. The revenue share note structure — rather than equity — reflects a deliberate choice to offer investors a yield-bearing instrument tied directly to operational cash flow rather than a speculative equity outcome.</p>
  <p>I want to be direct about what this investment is: it is not a growth bet. It is a bet that LastMile builds a durable regional utility, generates predictable operating cash flows, and services the note from those flows. If they don't build the network they've projected or don't achieve the customer penetration they've modeled, you don't get paid. I've underwritten the customer density and the deployment cost assumptions independently and I believe they're achievable. I am investing $200,000 because I believe that.</p>
  <div class="thesis-sig">— Jim Kowalski, Former VP Operations, Frontier Communications &nbsp;·&nbsp; Investing $200,000 on these terms</div>
</div>

<div class="deal-section">
  <span class="deal-section-label">Why this deal exists here</span>
  <h2>Why LastMile is raising on Unaccredited</h2>
  <p>Rural broadband infrastructure is not a venture capital asset. The return profile doesn't produce the fund-returning outcomes that VC math requires. It produces a 12–18% yield on a 5-year note, which is exactly what it says it is: a fixed-income alternative for investors who want yield and can tolerate the liquidity constraints of a private instrument. The investors most likely to understand and value this product are people who live in the communities LastMile serves — people who have direct experience with the problem the company is solving and who have a non-financial stake in the outcome.</p>
  <p>We listed this deal because: (1) Jim Kowalski is investing $200,000 of his personal capital — not an institutional check, not a nominal signaling investment; (2) the revenue share structure is explicitly a debt instrument, not equity dressed up as yield, and the risk profile is disclosed accordingly; and (3) LastMile's network deployment plan and customer density assumptions have been reviewed by the lead investor with 22 years of direct operational experience in the sector. We are investing $50,000 of platform capital in this note. If LastMile fails to service the note, we are a creditor alongside you.</p>

  <h2>What can go wrong</h2>
  <p style="font-size:0.88rem; opacity:0.75; max-width:60ch;">These are not boilerplate. They are the specific risks we think are most likely to matter for this investment.</p>
</div>

<div class="risk-section">
  <ol class="risk-list">
    <li class="risk-item">You can lose all of your money. LastMile is a pre-revenue-scale startup building physical network infrastructure in geographically challenging terrain. If deployment costs exceed projections, if customer penetration is materially below model, or if the company fails to reach operating cash flow before its working capital runway runs out, you may receive partial revenue share payments and no principal repayment.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">Revenue-share notes are not bonds. The return on this note is variable and directly tied to LastMile's gross revenue. There is no fixed interest rate and no guaranteed minimum payment. If revenue is lower than projected, payments are proportionally lower. At materially below-model revenue, principal may not be repaid at maturity.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">This investment is illiquid. There is no secondary market for Reg CF revenue share notes. You cannot sell your position. The only liquidity event is note maturity or early payoff by the company — neither of which is guaranteed on any specific timeline.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">Deployment execution risk. The note proceeds fund network expansion into three new service areas. Physical infrastructure deployment involves permitting timelines, weather delays, supply chain dependencies, and subcontractor performance — all of which are outside the company's direct control. Delays extend the timeline before note service payments begin.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">Competitive overbuild risk. Federal BEAD program funding may enable a larger competitor to deploy in LastMile's service areas after the company has established coverage. A well-funded overbuild could compress penetration rates and reduce the revenue that services this note, even if LastMile's network is operationally superior.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">Regulatory and spectrum risk. LastMile's network relies on licensed spectrum that is subject to FCC regulation. Regulatory changes, interference disputes, or license revocation could impair the company's ability to operate. Rural broadband policy is an active legislative and regulatory area, which creates both opportunity and uncertainty.</li>
    <li class="risk-item">The lead investor is not your fiduciary. Jim Kowalski's interest in LastMile's success is aligned with yours as a co-holder of the same note instrument. However, his operational expertise may lead him to assess the deployment risks differently than you would, and his capital position allows him to accept credit risk that may be inappropriate at smaller investment sizes.</li>
  </ol>
