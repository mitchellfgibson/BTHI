    <h2>Why this matters to me</h2>
  </div>

  <p>The intersection of capital markets and political economy is where I've spent most of my academic energy. At LSE I studied how different countries structure access to capital — why Germany's Mittelstand depends on relationship banking while the US tech ecosystem runs on venture equity, and why those aren't just cultural preferences but outcomes of regulatory architecture.</p>

  <p>Retail alternative investments are a microcosm of a larger political economy question: who gets access to the assets that generate wealth, on what terms, and why? The standard answer — "accredited investors have access because they're sophisticated" — doesn't hold up to scrutiny. The accreditation threshold is an income/wealth test, not a sophistication test. The correlation between being wealthy and being a good investor is weak. The regulatory structure mostly exists to protect the industry from liability, not to protect investors from loss.</p>

  <div class="pull-quote">
    <p>"The crowdfunding industry's mismatch between its marketing — 'democratizing access to investment' — and its mechanics — retail investors getting worse terms, worse information, and worse exits — is the kind of structural critique I find analytically interesting and morally worth addressing."</p>
  </div>

  <p>BTHI's positioning is interesting to me because it takes the "alongside experienced investors on the same terms" thesis seriously, rather than gesturing at democratization while quietly reproducing the same structural disadvantages. Whether that thesis is executable within the regulatory framework is the genuinely hard question — and the kind of question I want to work on.</p>
</section>
