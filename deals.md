<p>This isn't a behavioral finance argument. It's not about investors making emotional decisions or failing to diversify. These are structural problems — embedded in the legal architecture, the platform incentive stack, and the deal mechanics themselves — that would disadvantage a rational, well-informed retail investor even if they did everything right.</p>

<p>Ten mechanics. In roughly the order they compound on each other.</p>

<hr class="rule">

<ol class="breakdown-list">

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">Information Asymmetry</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">The company raising capital on a Reg CF platform has been working with its counsel and advisors for months to structure the offering. The investor gets a Form C filing and whatever the platform's marketing team chose to highlight. The filing is legally required to disclose material risks — but "material" is defined by the issuer's counsel, not an independent auditor. Financial projections are unaudited. The story is told by the person selling.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">In a venture context, an institutional investor spends weeks on diligence, calls references, models the unit economics, and still loses money frequently. The retail investor on a crowdfunding platform is working with a fraction of the information and rarely has the time or expertise to close the gap.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">Adverse Selection in Deal Flow</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">The best deals don't come to retail crowdfunding platforms. Why would they? A founder with genuine traction and strong investor interest can raise from VCs on better terms — no public disclosure requirement, no Reg CF compliance costs, no crowdfunding platform fees, and no crowd of small investors to manage on the cap table. The companies that turn to Reg CF crowdfunding are, systematically, the ones that couldn't raise from institutional sources.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">This isn't a rule — there are exceptions, and some founders genuinely prefer community ownership. But it's the base case. The selection effect means retail investors are competing for the deals that sophisticated money passed on.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">Valuation Premium</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">Reg CF deals are almost universally priced at post-money valuations that would be considered expensive by institutional venture standards. A pre-revenue company raising at a $10M valuation in a Reg CF round is not unusual. A seed-stage VC would price the same company at $3–6M and negotiate hard on terms. The retail investor pays the premium — justified by the story, not the fundamentals — and the margin of safety that valuation discipline provides disappears.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">The platform has no incentive to push back on valuation. Their revenue is a percentage of capital raised. Higher valuation → less dilution to the founder → easier fundraise → more capital raised → higher platform fee.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">Terms: SAFEs Without the Side Letters</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">Most Reg CF raises use SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity). SAFEs are fine instruments. But in a venture context, sophisticated investors negotiating SAFEs negotiate side letters — pro-rata rights (the right to participate in future rounds), information rights (quarterly financials), and MFN provisions (most favored nation, ensuring your terms aren't worse than any other investor's).</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">Retail crowdfunding investors get the SAFE without the side letters. Their conversion happens at the same trigger events, but they have no contractual right to invest in the next round, no guarantee they'll see financials unless voluntarily provided, and no protection against the company giving better terms to institutional investors who come in later.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">Dilution Over Time</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">A SAFE converts to equity at the next priced round — but the retail investor has no pro-rata rights, so their ownership percentage is immediately diluted by any new investors. If the company raises a seed round, then a Series A, then a Series B, the original Reg CF investor's stake is diluted at each step with no ability to maintain their percentage. The people who got in first and cheapest are, paradoxically, the ones most exposed to dilution.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">Institutional investors at each subsequent round negotiate pro-rata rights as a standard term. The crowd investor watches their percentage shrink with each success story.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">No Liquidity</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">There is no functional secondary market for Reg CF equity. StartEngine Secondary exists but volume is thin and bid-ask spreads are wide. The practical reality: you're investing in an instrument that you cannot sell for 7–10 years — if ever. Companies rarely go public. M&A exits happen but are unpredictable. The crowd investor's only exit is an event entirely outside their control, on a timeline they cannot predict.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">Institutional investors in the same company have negotiated drag-along provisions, co-sale rights, and registration rights that give them levers at exit. The retail investor has none of these.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">No Governance, No Information Rights</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">A $500 investment in a Reg CF round doesn't come with a board seat. It often doesn't come with observer rights. In most cases, the investor has no contractual right to receive updates — no obligation on the company to provide annual financials beyond the minimum Reg CF requirement, no right to call a shareholder meeting, no right to inspect the books.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">This is a feature, not a bug, from the company's perspective — they want capital without governance overhead. But it means retail investors have no mechanism to monitor the investment, flag concerns, or protect their position when something goes wrong.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">Cap Table Treatment: SPV Pooling</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">To avoid putting hundreds of individual retail investors directly on a company's cap table — which would create administrative nightmares and potentially trigger SEC reporting requirements — most platforms pool crowd investors into a single SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle). The SPV holds the equity; investors hold membership interests in the SPV.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">The practical consequence: the SPV manager — usually the platform — controls the SPV's voting. The individual investors vote for the SPV manager to vote on their behalf, but the SPV manager's interests are not always aligned with investors'. SPV fees further erode returns. And when the company and its institutional investors are negotiating terms at a future exit, the crowd SPV is a single line item with no leverage and a manager who may not have the time or incentive to fight for the best outcome.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">Platform Incentive Misalignment</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">Every major retail crowdfunding platform makes money on deal flow, not on deal outcomes. Wefunder charges issuers 7.9% of capital raised plus a payment processing fee. StartEngine charges 7–12% depending on campaign structure. The platform is paid when the round closes — before a single dollar of investor capital has been deployed, before any outcome is knowable.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">This creates an incentive to maximize the number of completed raises, not to curate for quality. A platform that turned down low-quality deals would raise less capital and earn less revenue. The platform's fiduciary interest, to the extent it exists legally, runs to shareholders — not to the retail investors using the marketplace.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

  <li class="breakdown-item">
    <div>
      <div class="breakdown-title">The Base Rate Math</div>
      <p class="breakdown-body">Institutional venture capital loses money on the majority of individual investments. The top-quartile VC firms generate returns because a small number of outlier outcomes (10–100x) offset the many zeros. The strategy works when you can build a portfolio of 30–50 investments, maintain pro-rata in the winners, and write off the losers at capital gains treatment.</p>
      <p class="breakdown-body" style="margin-top: 0.75rem;">The retail crowdfunding investor cannot replicate this. Investment minimums (typically $500–$2,500) combined with the $2,500 annual Reg CF investment limit for lower-income investors means meaningful portfolio construction is impossible. The investor who puts $500 into ten Reg CF deals isn't running a venture strategy — they're buying lottery tickets with worse odds and no diversification math working in their favor. Even if they pick correctly, they probably can't follow on into the winners.</p>
    </div>
  </li>

</ol>

<div class="callout callout-red">
  <div class="callout-label">The opportunity for BTHI</div>
  <h3>Fix three of these and you have a defensible product</h3>
  <p>Not all ten of these structural problems are solvable within the current regulatory framework. But several are — and solving even a targeted subset produces a meaningfully better product than anything currently on the market.</p>
  <ul class="white-space-list">
    <li><strong>Adverse selection:</strong> Fix by being the lead investor with skin in the game, sourcing deals the crowd can't find, and only listing deals you'd put your own capital into.</li>
    <li><strong>Information asymmetry:</strong> Fix with genuine diligence, published deal memos, and ongoing portfolio monitoring — not a Form C and a marketing video.</li>
    <li><strong>Platform incentive misalignment:</strong> Fix by aligning revenue to outcomes, not to raise completion. Carry on exits, not fees on closes.</li>
    <li><strong>SPV pooling opacity:</strong> Fix with transparent SPV terms, investor-elected SPV managers, and clear vote delegation rules disclosed upfront.</li>
    <li><strong>Base rate math:</strong> Fix with education — help investors understand portfolio construction, position sizing, and why this is a long-horizon, small-allocation asset class.</li>
  </ul>
  <p>The regulatory constraints on non-accredited investors are real. But the architecture of a platform that respects those constraints while fixing the structural disadvantages is buildable. That's the design problem worth solving.</p>
</div>
